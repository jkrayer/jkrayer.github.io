Foreward

Due to a conflict between a vacation and the delivery of my pre-order of the 5th Edition Player’s handbook I wound up at the Gamer’s Gambit in Saddlebrook, NJ. My aim was simply to get a pre-release copy to read on vacation but I left with an invitation to play at the store.

Thus began my second love affair with Dungeons and Dragons.

I’d unintentionally taken a long hiatus, of perhaps 15 years, from playing D&D as a serious hobby and thought (rather naively) I could pick up where I had left off. The old muscles had atrophied from disuse however, but with the patience of new friends they slowly redeveloped.

As the old skills returned so did the desire to tinker with the rules. For various reasons this can be more difficult in 5E. Out of frustration I began to look back at the D&D of my youth and discover (admittedly late) the OSR.

Time passed, the unspecified plague of unknown origin ended in-person play and dispersed friends, isolated in their homes, were looking for entertainment. A new group formed and in this environment the opportunity to experiment with different games emerged.

It was here that I began to analyze TSR era D&D and its retro-clones to find a system more satisfying to plan and to run. The analysis wasn’t easy. I’m strongly drawn to the simplicity of the various basic boxes of D&D and to Old School Essentials. I have a large amount of nostalgia for 2E AD&D. In the end I decided on AD&D. Not because I think AD&D is perfect. Not because I love seeing “E. Gary Gygax” on the covers of my books; which I do. But because I felt then, as I do now, that AD&D is a strong foundation to build a game on.

With one modern AD&D game behind me and a lot of time to consider my next move I’ve decided (because I couldn’t stop myself) to follow in the footsteps of so many fools. This is the start of my quixotical quest for the “Fantasy Heartbreaker.” The perfect game that makes the DM and all of his players happy.

The aim is a simplified game that’s fun to play, that can be easily modified to suit each new campaign but that remains AD&D at the core. I could go into a great deal of depth as to why but the very short reason is the ability to play a lot of wonderful adventures written for TSR era D&D with little or no modification.

All who participate in the game are encouraged to contribute to its evolution. I hope for this document to be the first step to a more fun, more customized game and I look forward to playing with all of you.

Jim
June 30, 2025

Introduction

The document you’re reading now extends and supersedes the AD&D Players Handbook. In some places it alters tables or simplifies or changes rules. In other places it moves rules from the AD&D Dungeon Master’s guide for the sake of clarity. Having the AD&D Players Handbook available during play is helpful but not strictly necessary. The OSRIC Compendium may also be used.

These rules may be revised at any time for consistency and clarity.

Creating a Character

Roll Ability Scores

Roll 4d6 six times. Remove the lowest die from each roll. For Example, if  a 6, 4, 1 and 3 were rolled remove the 1 die and sum the remaining dice for the final score, 13. This rolling method will produce above average characters.

Mulligan. If 2 or more scores of 6 or less or 4 or more scores of 9 or less scrap this character and start again.

Choose Race and Class

Arrange your scores to best suit the race and class of your choice. Some classes are difficult to roll for due to their relative rarity in the game world.

Alignment

Alignment is a broad description of the character’s philosophy not a description of personality or  a prescription for the character’s behavior. It does, however,  dictate to some extent how the character interacts with the world.

Alignment is designated along two axes, This first is Law and Chaos. The second is Good and Evil.

The Good and Evil axis is the simpler of the two. Good characters value life and act to preserve it. Evil characters place little value on the lives of others and would certainly never sacrifice their own.

Law may be thought of as order. Lawful characters operate according to  a code, whether it be personal, professional or religious. Chaos means operating according to personal whim. Lawful characters make decisions based on principles while Chaotic characters tend to do what feels right in the moment with little regard for consistency and often consequences.

The game world operates according to these philosophies. Monsters, gods, cultures and some highly powerful magical-items are intrinsically tied to some combination of Law/Chaos and Good/Evil. The personal philosophies of the characters dictate their relationship to these forces and serve a guide to their choices.

Races

All character races may choose to be single classed with no level limits. Non-human races may choose to multi-class with level limits. Each entry specifies allowable class combinations and level limits.

Each entry begins with a list of features. Age, lifespan, height and weight may be rolled or simply used as a guide when creating characters.

Each non-human race knows its own language and Common. If the character receives additional languages due to high Intelligence they should select additional languages from the provided list first.

Character Classes

Assassin

Minimum Scores: Str 12, Int 11, Dex 12
Hit Die Type: d6 (max 10 hit dice)
Alignment: Any Neutral or Evil
Race: Any
Armor/Shield: Leather and Studded Leather
Weapons: Club, dagger, dart, oil, sling, single-handed swords (except bastard swords), crossbows
Special: Open Locks, Move Silently, Hide In Shadows, Climb Walls, Eavesdrop, Read Languages, Assassinate, Disguise, Poisons, Languages

At first level the Assassin character starts with 60 points to distribute among the sills Open Locks, Move Silently, Hide In Shadows, Climb Walls, Eavesdrop, Read Languages. No more than 30 points may be added to a single skill. Assassin characters gain an additional 22 points per level starting at level 2 and ending at level 20. The player may not assign any more than 11 points to any one skill at each of these levels. No skill may ever exceed 99%.

Open Locks may be attempted by the Assassin but once per lock per level. Locks themselves vary in difficulty with the best locks designed specifically to defeat tampering.

Move Silently you’re quiet but not invisible. Clever use of terrain or combining this skill others make it most effective. Success increases your chances to surprise enemies by 1.

Hide In Shadows by remaining perfectly still the Assassin may conceal themselves in an area of low light. Hide in Shadows never works if the Assassin is being directly observed and may be defeated by light and certain types of spells or otherworldly vision. As with Move Silently, Hide in Shadows increases your chances to surprise enemies by 1.

An Assassin may either be moving or hiding but never both. The bonus to Surprise granted by Move Silently and Hide in Shadows are never cumulative.

Climb Walls is used when the Assassin free climbs or when under fire. The first roll is made when the Assassin has climbed half the intended distance. Failure indicates a set back of some kind. Failure by more than 20% indicates the Assassin falls and takes 1d6 damage per 10 feet fallen. Further rolls will be required to complete the climb.

Eavesdrop (Detect Noise) is used most commonly when the Assassin wishes to hear a sound that may otherwise be muffled due to distance, background noise or because of an interposing barrier such as a door. The Assassin may also use this skill to read lips. The Assassin must be able to understand the language being spoken when using eavesdrop in this way.

Read Languages is a measure of the Assassin’s ability to use their current knowledge of language to puzzle out the written word in languages they do not know. Languages adjacent to those known by the Assassin are easier to figure out than distant languages. Some may be far too foreign for the Assassin to read.

Assassinate is used whenever the Assassin succeeds a to hit-roll for back-stab and before damage is rolled. The player has a percent chance to kill their target based on their level +/- 5% multiplied by the difference between the Assassin's level and the target's. For example a 5th level Assassin attacking a 3rd level target would have an 80% chance to succeed. Base 70% + (5 - 3) * 5 = 80%. The same Assassin attacking a 7th level target would have a 60% chance to succeed. Base 70% + (5 - 7) * 5 = 60%. If the the Assassinate roll is successful, the target is instantly killed. If it fails, back-stab damage is rolled as normal.

Assassins may Disguise themselves to appear as any similar creature of either sex. Disguise may alter their height by +/- 5 inches and make the Assassin look slimmer or appear much heavier. Disguise can make the assassin appear to be virtually any class of character, a simple pilgrim, a merchant, etc.

Assassins who disguise themselves as generic creatures such as "Guard" have a small chance of being spotted. When attempting to disguise themselves as a specific individual the chance of being spotted increases significantly. The DM will calculate the chance of discovery and roll in secret. Careful planning and execution can help mitigate this risk.

Poison. Assassins are skilled in the application of ingested and insinuated poisons. Those able to find an alchemist both willing and able to manufacture such concoction may use any skillfully. Others learn to specialize in the creation and application of a single poison either by raising animals whose venom can be harvested or plants whose extracts can be refined. Assasins gain this feature at 7th Level.

Languages. The Assassin may learn languages in addition to the those gained from having a high Intelligence score. Languages gained from this feature are a reflection of the Assassin’s facility with language. The Assassin has picked these up conversationally rather than formally and may not be able to read or write them. Languages chosen from this feature must be currently spoken languages.

Bard

Minimum Scores: Dex 12, Int 13, Cha 15
Hit Die Type: d6 (max 10 hit dice)
Alignment: Any Neutral
Race: Half-Elf, Human
Armour/Shield: Leather and Studded Leather
Weapons: Club, dagger, dart, oil, sling, single-handed swords (except bastard swords)
Special: Pick Pockets, Climb Walls, Eavesdrop, Read Languages, Influence, Inspire, Counter, Lore, Spells.

At first level the Bard character starts with 26 points to distribute among the skills Pick Pockets, Climb Walls, Eavesdrop and Read Languages, no more than 13 points may be added to a single skill. Bard characters gain an additional 15 points per level starting at level 2 and ending at level 20. The player may not assign any more than 8 points to any one skill at each of these levels. No skill may ever exceed 99%. The skills Influence, Inspire, Counter and Lore work as described below.

Pick Pockets is the skill used to lift (or plant) small personal items from unsuspecting marks. The Thief may access pouches, pockets or even loose jewelry but may not obtain a stowed item. If the thief fails their roll by more than 20% they’ll be noticed by their target or one of his allies.

Climb Walls is used when the thief free climbs or when under fire. The first roll is made when the thief has climbed half the intended distance. Failure indicates a set back of some kind. Failure by more than 20% indicates the thief falls and takes 1d6 damage per 10 feet fallen. Further rolls will be required to complete the climb.

Eavesdrop (Detect Noise) is used most commonly when the Bard wishes to hear a sound that may otherwise be muffled due to distance, background noise or because of an interposing barrier such as a door. The Bard may also use this skill to read lips. The Bard must be able to understand the language being spoken when using eavesdrop in this way.

Read Languages is a measure of the Bard’s ability to use their current knowledge of language to puzzle out the written word in languages they do not know. Languages adjacent to those known by the Bard are easier to figure out than distant languages. Some may be far too foreign for the Bard to read.

Influence The Bard may use the power of their Rock to influence the behavior of those who can hear shifting the crowd toward hostile, friendly or appropriate. Listeners save vs Paralyzation at -1 for every 3 levels of the Bard. A failed save shifts the crowd as the Bard desires, success shifts the crowd’s attitude in the opposite direction. This ability may not be used to influence anyone currently engaged in combat.

Inspire The Bard may use the power of their Rock to inspire those about to do battle. The Bard must know the nature of the threat and have at least 3 minutes to perform before battle begins. Those who listen are granted +1 to attack, +1 to saving throws and +2 to morale roles. This effect lasts for 1 round per level of the Bard. Those currently in combat may not be inspired this way.

Counter Once per encounter or battle the bard can use the power of their Rock to block incoming magical attacks or effects involving poetry or song or meant to influence characters through commands or charms. During this time the Bard may only perform and move at a slow walk. Allies within a 30’ radius receive this benefit. The Bard must save vs Spell to block these attacks. A failed save or damage from weapons or spells ends this effect.

Lore Bards pursue broad knowledge with a zeal unknown to others granting them knowledge of a great many subjects. Given 10 minutes to handle an item the Bard has a 5% chance per level (max 95%) to identify it. This is not a magical identification but instead a recollection of tales of the item, its owners and the feats they performed while carrying it. Great success grants the bard greater recall.

Acquiring New Spells Bards have notoriously short-attention spans rendering the kind of study required to be a Magic-User impossible. Any spells they gain from first to last are the product of happenstance. The DM will provide (or ask the player to select) one first level spell. Thereafter the Bard may only learn spells from scrolls or captured spell books and must spend time and resources to learn these in the same way as a Magic-User.

Cleric

Minimum Scores: Wis 9
Hit Die Type: d8 (max 9 hit dice)
Alignment: By Faith
Race: Dwarf, Gnome, Half-Elf, Halfling, Human
Armour/Shield: Any
Weapons: Melee and Thrown Bludgeoning
Special: Spells, Relics, Turn Undead, Deity Benefit

Acquiring New Spells In game terms we call the divine power of Clerics spells for the sake of keeping the rules simple. But in the game world Cleric’s pray to their respective gods and are granted the ability (by their god) to perform miracles.

In order for a Cleric to learn new prayers he or she should remain in good standing with their local temple which will in turn guide the Cleric’s religious study helping the Cleric to learn new prayers for 1st and 2nd level spells. Cleric’s of 5th level or higher will encounter a teacher of the DM’s design capable of instructing the Cleric in prayers for 3rd-5th level spells. A divine servant of the deity in question will contact the Cleric of 11th level in order to provide instruction for spells 6th level and higher.

Some Clerics answer the call of their deity to venture far from established temples. In this case the deity or one of its servants will see to the Cleric’s education.

Captured prayer books filled with the divine (or profane) prayers to other gods are of no value to Clerics. Additionally Cleric’s don’t have magical scrolls to learn from since prayers may be answered but not saved for later.

Clerics may however find and later make relics.

Relics At 7th level the Cleric gains the ability to imbue sacred materials with a fraction of divine power to create minor relics dedicated to their god. Think Prayer Beads.

At 12th level the Cleric gains the ability to imbue sacred materials with a portion of divine power to create major relics dedicated to their god. Think Holy Avenger.

Druid

Minimum Scores: Wis 12 Cha 15 (27)
Hit Die Type: d8 (max 9 hit dice)
Alignment: Any Good
Race: Elf, Half-Elf, Human
Armour/Shield: Any leather or wood.
Weapons: Any wooden or stone
Special: Spells, Fetishes

Druids in this game use the Cleric Experience and Spell tables from this volume. In all other regards Druids work exactly as described in the AD&D PHB excepting the rules limiting the number of Druids at a given level and having to challenge an existing Druid to attain that level. These rules are simply ignored.

Acquiring New Spells In game terms we call the primordial power of Druids spells for the sake of keeping the rules simple. But in the game world Druids’s tap into the primal forces of nature to perform miracles.

In order for the Druid to strengthen their connection to nature and thus increase their number of “spells” the druid needs to spend time reconnecting with nature in their native environment. During this time of meditation and reflection the Druid will find many teachers in the trees and the winds and mundane and magical creatures of the forest.

Druid power comes from their connection to nature. No written word can capture that connection, no ink inscribed on something dead can preserve it. Druids themselves may be (but are not required to be) completely illiterate. Druids therefore do not have scrolls but they may find and later make fetishes (see below).

Fetishes At 7th level the Druid gains the ability imbue natural materials with a fraction of primordial power to create minor fetishes.

At 12th level the Druid gains the ability to imbue natural materials with a portion of primordial power to create major relics. Think “Staff of the Woodlands”.

Weapon Specialization When creating a fighter the player may select a single melee weapon to be “specialized” with. Weapon specialization grants the fighter one extra attack every 2 rounds ( see fighter table “Melee Attacks” above) and +1 to hit and +2 damage owing to their skill with the chosen weapon.

Fighters with Dexterity higher than their Strength may instead choose a ranged weapon specialization. This benefit allows the fighter to add 1 to the weapon’s rate of fire and adds +1 to hit and +2 damage as above.

Weapon Mastery At 5th level the fighter is so practiced with their specialized weapon that they receive an additional +2 to hit and +1 to damage when using that weapon for a total of +3 to hit and +3 to damage.

Cleave Starting at first level when the fighter uses a weapon to kill an enemy with less than 1hd the fighter may make an additional attack against another creature also with less than 1 hd. The fighter may use this feature every round. The total attacks a Fighter can make in any given round including attacks granted by level or specialization may not exceed the Fighter’s current level with the exception that cleave always grants a minimum of 1 extra attack. This feature improves at levels 7 and 13.

To become a Knight the player character must have served under a more experienced Knight as both page (ages 8-13) and squire (ages 14 - 20+). When the mentoring Knight is satisfied with the completion of the player character’s training the mentor grants the PC the title “Knight.”

The 1st level Knight begins their career at the completion of this training as a Knight Errant or Adventuring Knight. The Player Character must then seek out great deeds to distinguish themselves among their peers and thereby receive an invitation to join a knightly order.

The knight may issue this challenge to any enemy but don’t mistake bravery for foolishness. Knights don’t expect casters, brigands or deceivers to honor the challenge.

Acquiring New Spells One who is a Magic-User is by nature the studious sort. It’s therefore assumed that Magic-User characters spend much of their free time in study and consideration of the nuance of each casting. Due to this constant study Magic-Users may gain a new spell when gaining a new level. Spells gained for advancing in levels need not be rolled for as with learning spells from scrolls and books (below) but they do count toward the maximum number of spells the magic-user may know. Spells learned in this way do not require days of study as with captured books and scrolls since they represent the culmination of prior days of study.

The Magic-User will need to spend 1 day per spell level to complete their study and fully integrate the knowledge. Scrolls used in this way are consumed.

Thief

Minimum Scores: Dex 9
Hit Die Type: d6 (max 10 hit dice)
Alignment: Any neutral or evil
Race: any
Armour/Shield: Leather and Studded Leather
Weapons: Club, dagger, dart, oil, sling, single-handed swords (except bastard swords)
Special: Backstab, Pick Pockets, Open Locks, Find/Remove Traps, Move Silently, Hide in Shadows, Climb Walls.

Thief characters have 7 skills that represent various aspects of their trade. The back-stab skill increases at the rate indicated in the above table while the remaining 6 skills are increased by the player. At first level the thief character starts with 60 points to distribute among their remaining 6 skills, no more than 30 points may be added to a single skill. Thief characters gain an additional 22 points per level starting at level 2 and ending at level 20. The player may not assign any more than 11 points to any one skill at each of these levels. No skill may ever exceed 99%.

Pick Pockets is the skill used to lift (or plant) small personal items from unsuspecting marks. The Thief may access pouches, pockets or even loose jewelry but may not obtain a stowed item. If the thief fails their roll by more than 20% they’ll be noticed by their target or one of his allies.

Open Locks may be attempted by the thief but once per lock per level. Locks themselves vary in difficulty with the best locks designed specifically to defeat tampering.

Thieves may spend time inspecting a lock before attempting to pick it. If the thief spends 1 turn inspecting a lock they may determine whether the lock is within their current skill and gain a 10% bonus to their Open Locks roll. The thief gains a 5% for each additional turn spent inspecting the lock to a maximum bonus of 20%. A thief may never have more than a 99% chance to succeed at opening a lock.

Finding and Removing Traps are two related functions that need to be rolled separately. Searching for a trap in this way requires the thief to spend 1 turn investigating the area suspected of being trapped. This area may be no larger than 10 square feet per turn of investigation. A failed find traps roll indicates the thief is satisfied no trap exists. Once a trap is found the thief may roll to remove the trap.

Any clever adventurer may find a trap without the need to role. Some traps are far too dangerous to disarm and should instead be thoughtfully avoided.

Move Silently you’re quiet but not invisible. Clever use of terrain or combining this skill others make it most effective. Success increases your chances to surprise enemies by 1.

Hide In Shadows by remaining perfectly still the thief may conceal themselves in an area of low light. Hide in Shadows never works if the thief is being directly observed and may be defeated by light and certain types of spells or otherworldly vision. As with Move Silently, Hide in Shadows increases your chances to surprise enemies by 1.

A Thief may either be moving or hiding but never both. The bonus to Surprise granted by Move Silently and Hide in Shadows are never cumulative.

Climb Walls is used when the thief free climbs or when under fire. The first roll is made when the thief has climbed half the intended distance. Failure indicates a set back of some kind. Failure by more than 20% indicates the thief falls and takes 1d6 damage per 10 feet fallen. Further rolls will be required to complete the climb.

Adventuring

Combat

Combat may begin in many ways but most commonly it begins when hostile forces meet in the field, when negotiation between two or more parties dissolves into violence, when one hostile force lays an ambush for another or, when two hostile forces stumble upon one another.

Initiative

When hostile forces meet in the field the players and monsters will roll initiative dice. The DM will roll one die for the monsters or may at his discretion roll 1 die for each monster group or significant monster. The character or monster with the highest roll starts combat. In the event that communication breaks down and combat erupts the first player or monster or monster group to break will go first. No initiative dice are rolled.

When the first actor in the combat completes their turn that individual will call on the next actor in the combat and so on until each player and monster has taken their turn. The last actor in the current round will pick the first actor in the next round. If the monsters act last in a given round they’ll always choose themselves to act first in the following round.

Surprise

When hostile forces stumble upon each other they roll surprise dice. If one side succeeds while the other fails that side receives a surprise round. If both sides tie for failure or success, surprise is bypassed and initiative is rolled for as above. When one side lays an ambush for the other only the side laying in ambush rolls for surprise. If the ambushing side has plenty of time to prepare or is able to give themselves some other advantage they may gain surprise without needing to roll. A cautious group, alert to surprise may never be automatically surprised.

Please note that surprise is indicated differently throughout the rules. Elves and Halflings for instance have a 4 in 6 chance to surprise enemies where Rangers may only be surprised on a 1 in 6. The DM will set the actual conditions of surprise at the time it occurs.

Weapons and Spells

Combat comes in three flavors, melee, missile and area of affect. Weapons are always swung, thrown or shot and classify as melee, missile or both in the case of thrown weapons. Spells may be melee (when the range is touch), missile if they affect a single target or area of effect when they affect all targets in an area.

When a combatant is in melee range they may only use melee weapons, thrown missiles or cast spells with a range of self or touch. Melee is too dynamic for missile weapons or ranged spells to be deployed. Combatants who fire missiles or cast spells into a melee should do so with great caution for failure to hit your target means you may hit an ally. The DM will calculate this chance based on the circumstance and instruct the player to roll accordingly.

Cover and Concealment

When being fired upon by the enemy it makes sense to to take cover behind impermeable barriers such as walls or to obscure the attackers line of sight by hiding in dense underbrush. Doing so confers an AC bonus to the evading party.

Cover Concealment
1/4 -2 -1
1/2 -4 -2
2/4 -7 -3
total -* -4
* Creatures behind total cover may not be hit by mundane missiles.

Cover is not a perfect defense. While projectiles of insufficient mass that must travel in a straight line may be blocked by cover it can’t protect against spells that are able to bypass it (by exploding in the air above it for example) or missiles of sufficient mass to destroy it, such as a boulder hurled by a trebuchet.

Concealment can take various forms. Dense fog may make it harder to spot a target but can’t stop the flight path of a missile. A dense hedgerow may also block vision but allow some some missiles (such as arrows) through while others (a thrown axe) may become effectively entangled.

Players and DMs should use their best judgment regarding cover and concealment.

Grenade Like Missiles

From time to time players and their adversaries may wish to hurl fragile containers containing flammable or toxic liquids such as oil, acid or holy water. The range for such missiles is 1/2/3 and ROF is 1. Most containers made to contain these fluids are sturdy and a save must be made to see if they break on impact but players may obtain containers of these fluids that will always break at the cost of making them more risky to transport.

Fluid Damage on Impact Damage on Subsequent Rounds Save
Acid 2d4 1d4 / 2 20
Holy Water * 2d4 1d4 / 2 20
Oil ** 2d6 1d6 / 1 18
* Holy Water can only damage creatures vulnerable to its touch.
** Oil must be flaming in order to cause damage.

Time

Time may be tracked in the game in different ways. Most often we track time using “game time.” Using game time we simply say that an amount of minutes, hours or days have passed and continue with the game. If the characters are making a simple safe journey lasting three days then three days for the characters may pass in the time it takes the DM to say, “three days later.” If the session needs to end when the characters are adventuring then game time might stand still until the start of the next session.

Travel is not always safe. Sometimes the characters are passing through dangerous territory or may need to travel quickly. In cases where travel has some element of danger it may become an adventure.

Players should remember that both allied and opposed factions are bound by the same time and physical laws as their characters. It’s often desirable (or unavoidable) to use game time to say “five weeks later” but doing so allows game factions 5 weeks to advance their goals.

Exploration Time

During exploration time passes using “exploration time”. Exploration time is a function of how much distance the characters can cover or what size area they can search in a given amount of time (see below). Generally speaking exploration time is divided into three paces slow, normal and fast where slow covers the least area with the most caution and fast the most area with the least caution.

Dungeon Exploration

Dungeon exploration is measured in a unit of time called the “Game Turn” or simply turn. A turn equals 10 minutes. Please note the duration of most light sources is measured in turns. Characters may move through the dungeon at one of three paces slow, normal and fast. Movement is always assumed to occur at the pace of the slowest character, players may always indicate otherwise.

Slow. Characters move at 10’ x movement rate. When doing so they may detect approaching enemies before being detected themselves. Traps may be noticed without rolling and Find Traps rolls are done with a 20% bonus. Known traps are easily avoided.

Normal. Characters move at 20’ x movement rate. When doing so normal rules for surprise apply. Traps may only be noticed by rolling Find Traps. Known traps may or may not be stumbled over depending on their nature and prior precautions. For example a clearly indicated pressure plate in a wide hallway may be avoided while a trip wire that must be stepped over would require a check to see if a character makes a mis-step.

Fast. Characters move at 30’ x movement rate. When doing so characters will always be surprised by enemies. Traps may only be noticed by rolling Find Traps and this check is made with a 20% penalty. Characters have a chance to stumble over known traps no matter what precautions have been taken.

Monsters are subject to the same rules of movement. Their pace may modify the above rules. Generally speaking parties moving at a slower pace have an advantage over those moving at a faster pace while parties moving at the same pace cancel advantages.

Any party moving through its home is considered to have the “home court” advantage. This party is considered to be moving one pace slower to reflect this advantage.

Other Exploration Activities

These activities take 1 turn to accomplish.

Activity Notes
Combat* fight, search the bodies, take a breather
Search** 1 turn / 10 sq. ft. / no. of searchers
Find a trap 1 turn / 10 sq. ft.
Remove a trap the trap must be detected first
* 10 rounds of combat equals 1 turn. Characters must rest 1 additional turn for each turn of combat that goes beyond 9 rounds.
** 6 searchers can search 60 square feet in one turn.
Rest

Characters must take 1 turn of rest for every 5 turns of exploration or suffer a -1 penalty to hit until they rest.

Wandering Monsters

Monsters traverse dangerous environments at unpredictable intervals. The DM checks to see if these encounters occur at an interval of turns appropriate to the environment. Players should therefore spend turns with care.

Wilderness Exploration

As dungeon exploration is measured in feet and turns, wilderness exploration is measured in leagues and hours. 1 league is equal to 3 miles.

Terrain Hours/League Leagues / Day
Roads 1 8
Hills/Plains 2 4
Bog or Forest 4 2
Mountains 1 day 1

While these environments may be traversed at various rates (see Dungeon Exploration above) those rates can be affected by vehicles, mounts, magic and possibly equipment. Given that complexity the DM will fill in rules as necessary.

Searching a league of wilderness costs an amount of time equal to the time it takes to traverse that terrain. For example, searching 1 league of forest costs 4 hours.

Down Time

There will be times during the course of play that will naturally lead to the characters needing to take a break from adventuring. Spell casters need time to learn new spells and any character may need to take rest to recover from grievous wounds. The players may also decide that their characters simply wish to take some time off from questing in order to spend more time with their treasure.

No matter the case this is a time for the players to consider what their characters may wish to achieve outside of the adventure. A Thief may wish to find a fence, a Knight may search for a squire and the Fighter may simply wish to imbibe and be merry. A character may even wish to hire a group of adventurers to send off to achieve some goal the player characters don’t have time to address.

Any character may achieve or make progress toward achieving any goal by communicating it with the DM. Simple goals may need no more investment than the mere statement of intent while others may require additional in-game interactions to be carried out by the player and DM individually.
