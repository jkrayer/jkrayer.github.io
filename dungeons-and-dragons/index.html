Proposal: Thieves of Capitol

September 7, 2025

500 years before the war with the Elves during the reign of Emperor Leto, the Undying a group of thieves tries to make a name for themselves in the bustling streets of Capitol1. In this first chapter the player's fledgling crew will become embroiled in a murder plot that they must resolve to survive.

Abstract

Start Date: T.B.D.

Structure: At the end of each adventure the players will be presented with two jobs2 and must select one. The DM will prepare that job for the following session. All jobs2 will be offered by the patron of the crew, Joey Silver-Thumbs.

Sessions: 5 - 7. I will do my best to keep each session to a single evening's play.

Session 0: Characters will be created, questions answered and the first two jobs2 presented.

Pregenerated Characters: See Below

Experience: Special rules for awarding experience to emphasize mission and objective completion over combat.

Special Equipment: Sap. A successful back-stab attack made with a sap renders the target unconscious. The target must have hit dice less than or equal to the wielders back-stab multiplier, be a medium sized or smaller humanoid and must not be wearing metal armor. The target is rendered unconscious for d4 + back-stab multiplier rounds, d4 + 2 for a first level Thief. A natural 20 doubles the duration.

Reputation: Reputation with other factions starts at 0 (unknown) and may be adjusted up or down by 1-2 points after each mission.

Conclusion: At the end of this Chapter a second chapter or new campaign may be proposed.

Play test: The Assassin, Bard and adjustments to the Fighter and Thief abilities will be tested for balance and fun.

Adventures in the City, Maybe

August 2, 2025

My first instinct while I was writing the last was to create games that allow me to both play test rules and develop parts of the setting through running games. This development is not merely geographic but also temporal. I think giving the players the chance to learn about the setting through time and space will give them cross campaign insights. For my part it gives me the opportunity to find the place and time in which they'll have the most fun.

Capitol: a City in Need of a Name

My earliest game 5E with the members of my current group took place in my home brew Nentir Vale setting. Nentir Vale is from 4E and much of the customization I made and shared poorly was a reaction to “Humans in Funny Hats” combined with the “Gods of Your World” section of the 5E Dungeon Master's Guide. Adventures on the frontier of a fallen empire are simple enough since they can be for the most part standard D&D adventures. It begs the question, “Why did the Empire fall and what was it like?”

The answers could be explored in the form of a lore document but I don't expect my players to be invested enough to read that sort of document. I believe it's far more rewarding for both myself and the players to learn about the Capitol through play. That means city adventure.

I am, for certain, intimidated by the idea of running a city adventure but, it could also be a lot of fun. To pull it off though I need a lot of advice. After some searching I've narrowed my basic resources to two to prevent analysis paralysis; “Cities of Mystery” and “Blades in the Dark”. “Cities of Mystery” because it's a great city building resource and “Blades in the Dark” because D&D a city based game needs non-combat systems to resolve, suspense and so forth. Oh, did I forget to mention I plan for this to be a "thieves" campaign?

Planning Capitol: a City that Still Needs a Name

I glanced over a lot of wonderful advice from The Alexandrian that I'll probably return to but I'm often an impatient sort so I sketched out my own plan.

  1. Map the City
  2. Map the Neighborhoods
  3. Insert Important Landmarks, city buildings, religious buildings, monuments.
  4. Map the starting Neighborhood in detail.
  5. Actually write some adventures because that's the part the players actually care about.
  6. Name the City, don't forget to name the city.

That's basically it. I'll of course need to produce a brief setting guide since certain classes won't work in a thieving campaign. The players should also know what to expect in a game so they can create characters they'll enjoy playing throughout.

Adventures in Capitol: a Joke that's Getting Old

D&D is a great framework for general fantasy adventures but when one wants to run more specialized games additional rules are needed to reward a specialized style of play. Combat is the primary stressor for players but in a city with guards and punitive laws avoiding it would be the better course of action. So we need some a framework to for stealth based adventure design and a system for rewarding players for stealth and planning.

Fortunately some very talented people have solved this problem in the past. Let us therefore mine “Blades in the Dark” for that which we can use in D&D.

Thoughts About My Next Game

July 26, 2025

I‘ve been working in fits and starts to prepare good old “T1-4 The Temple of Elemental Evil” for my next game. I reasoned a while back that preparing a game in the "World of Greyhawk" would be both rewarding and less difficult than writing my own game. And yet I can't shake the nagging feeling that I should be working on my own setting.

The why of it is pretty simple. As much as I love the D&D of my youth I find the settings a bit bland. It‘s the old “Humans in Funny Hats” canard. If Elfs and Dwarfs and so forth don't have a unique perspective on the world or meaningful cultural distinctions from humans then they are no different from humans.

And I quite think the point applies elsewhere. Should all Clerics have power over the undead? Do all gods have Paladins? Do Clerics and Paladins make sense in a fantasy world missing the Christian context these classes seem to belong to? Should every race worship the same way? Should fantasy religions be divided by race or by geography as real world mythology?

I, of course, wouldn‘t be asking these questions if I was happy with the way things are.

On the other hand I do want to get back to running games. Doing so in a world of my own invention would be far more difficult than preparing a module. Sadly my heart just isn't in it, module preparation that is.

So the right thing to do is explore some ideas that might hold my interest and build around that regardless of effort.
