Batch #3: Neshanic Valley Dutch Clover

Part of Experimentation Matrix 1

Previous to this batch I made two batches using the Blue Ox Deluxe Mead Making Kit and honey from Costco. Mistakes were made, first among them the honey.

Since then I've read "The Complete Guide to Making Mead" by Steve Platz. Using the information in this book I can now set a goal in terms of alcohol by volume and sweetness.

A sack strength semi-sweet mead.

Assumptions

I expect yeast will die at or minimally above its alcohol tolerance. Based on that assumption I've set my target starting and ending gravity readings to produce a 15% semi-sweet mead.

Ingredients

  • 100 oz Poland Spring Water
  • 3 pounds Neshanic Valley Dutch Clover Honey
  • 5 grams Lalvin ICV D47 Yeast

Observations

Mixed must in a pitcher with a spoon instead of shaking 1 gallon carboy. This went very well. A bigger pitcher would reduce spillage.

Only need about 100 oz of water to rehydrate yeast and make the must.

Clover honey was a massive improvement over Costco honey.

Must color is far darker also due to the honey.

A lot of up front CO2 led to early explosions. Degass more carefully in the future.

Did not adjust gravity readings for temperature but considering the adjustments are by thousandths I think the impact is negligible as long as the temperature is stable.

Cold-crashing and racking seems to have worked bust also resulted in a lot of waste. For future batches I plan to back-sweeten as many times as necessary for the yeast to die of natural causes and for the desired final gravity to be reached.

Loss as 1 gallon feels tragic. I'll stick with 1 gallon batches for experimentation but definitely want to move on to 3 or 5 gallon batches.

Day of Bottling

Pale yellow, cloudy, dry yeasty flavor but doesn't leave the mouth feeling dry.

This leads me to believe "green" Mead should be aged.
