Researching Stabilization

August 3, 2025

After cold-crashing Batch #3 I've done a lot of research about halting fermentation. The common wisdom seems to be that cold-crashing is the best way to create bottle-bombs1 and I certainly don't have enough refrigerator space to store increasing amounts of mead in the fridge. Since that's the case I need a solution for storing Mead at room temperature. And to make my life difficult I prefer not to fortify in order to keep alcohol levels sane and I prefer not to use sulfites.

This leaves pasteurization. So when my new shipment of sanitizer comes will sanitize and pasteurize Batch #4. I could not get more of this honey and don't wish to mix honeys (yet) for back sweetening so I'm going to let Batch #4 go dry and see what happens.

Due to earlier suppositions about the rate of experimentation Batch #5 is hot on its heals. I racked off the primary yesterday and I'm already seeing more yeast fall to the bottom so it seems I'll need to pasteurization this batch too without gathering any data from Batch #4. On second thought I've got more of this honey so perhaps I'll back-sweeten with what's left and see what happens.

It makes sense, therefore, to delay beginning Batch #6 until I've succeeded or failed with pasteurizing these two batches. Here I'll have to adjust my process once again. I'll target a starting gravity of 1.115 in order to yield 15% alcohol at 1.000 final gravity, the pasteurize and back back-sweeten. Batch #6 may actually turn out to be my first attempt at pasteurization.

From here I could make Batch #6 the final round in Matrix 1 since I feel I'll have effectively perfected my process. But I would like to stick with Matrix 1 for 2-3 more batches. I think I've yet to use Butterbean and Orange Blossom. This is true but they're not available. I purchased Cranberry Blossom and Alfalfa Blossom instead.

Matrix 2 will be a lower ABV Mead perhaps Hydromel or Standard strength. I'm hoping for some Meads that I don't need to age as long so I can enjoy them sooner. I was able to purchase 5 lbs of Alfalfa Blossom honey so I'm wondering if I can get a semi-sweet hydromel strength and sack strength Mead out of that amount.

Observations After Bottling Batch #3

July 26, 2025

Bottled and drank Batch #3 last night. It was Mead but still yeasty and cloudy. I, therefore, feel it's best to age future batches a minimum of three months.

With this in mind I need to plan around bottle aging or aging in the carboy. Bottle aging frees up carboys but could result in differences in flavor in the same batch over time. For my level of production this is probably not a meaningful concern.

Additionally I've worked through about half of the locally available honeys.

Given my plan to age for a minimum of 3 months and my progression through locally available honeys I think it make sense to slow Matrix 1 down to monthly. This should give me plenty of time to get each batch stable in the carboy before bottling and a new mead to try one each month.

Batches #4 and #5 were started within a fortnight of each other. I'll simply age these a minimum of three months and have two batches close to one another.

Was also reading about de-gassing immediately prior to starting Batch #5. I saw recommendations to stop after 3 days. I've been observing a rapid drop in CO2 during the first weeks while bubbling continues to to happen every second. So a reductions from 7 to 3 days may be in order.

Why Mead?

July 18, 2025

Earlier this year (2025) I promised myself to say "yes" to any crazy idea that popped into my head and then pursue that idea to completion. My first foray into Mead making was not great—my own fault—but the spirit of my promise had been achieved and I could have left Mead making there.

My first 2 batches left me with a question that I've often asked elsewhere in my life. "How does one improve a skill?"

Consider that in learning to make Mead as in other areas of life I have books and goals but not direct mentors, or in this case, natural talent. I've met people with sophisticated palettes and know for certain I'm not among them.

I am, however, having fun and feel I can devise a procedure to learn this skill which, I hope, will be applicable to future endeavors.

To this end I've devised an experimentation matrix to allow me to learn what flavors different honeys and yeasts produce. The goal is to limit variability along two axes (yeast and honey) and produce different test batches that will both help me refine my process and learn—to the best of my limited ability—what flavors I can produce.

Week 1 Week 2 Week 3 Week 4 Week 5 Week 6 Week 7 Week 8
Batch #1 Rest Rest Rest Bottle
Batch #2 Rest Rest Rest Bottle
Batch #3 Rest Rest Rest
Batch #4 Rest

If money, space and equipment were of no concern odd weeks would be for honey experiments and even weeks yeast experiments. I'd start a new one gallon batch every week with odd weeks using different honey with the same yeast and even weeks same honey different yeast.

In reality these resources are all limited. Local honeys may be obtained in 5-10 varieties but testing the sheer number of yeast strains would be prohibitively expensive.

Additionally this is a fairly aggressive experimentation pace if one also wants to age their Mead for any significant period. Considering I'm targeting sack strength Meads (an arbitrary target to be sure) it seems aging should take place for anywhere between 3 and 24 months.

This would no doubt lead to a lot of 1 gallon jugs aging in the basement which while fun would not be an efficient use of equipment. Admittedly 1 gallon jugs are not prohibitively expensive but at some point moving on to fermenting larger quantities of Mead would be more practical.

The plan therefore is to proceed with a new batch every other week experimenting with honey while keeping yeast the same. The experiments will be bottle aged and consumed at intervals of bottle date, 3 months and 6 months. This supposes I'll consistently produce 1 gallon batches.

This plan will of course be revised along the way.
