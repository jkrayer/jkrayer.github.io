Why Mead?

July 18, 2025

Earlier this year (2025) I promised myself to say "yes" to any crazy idea that popped into my head and then pursue that idea to completion. My first foray into Mead making was not great—my own fault—but the spirit of my promise had been achieved and I could have left Mead making there.

My first 2 batches left me with a question that I've often asked elsewhere in my life. "How does one improve a skill?"

Consider that in learning to make Mead as in other areas of life I have books and goals but not direct mentors, or in this case, natural talent. I've met people with sophisticated palettes and know for certain I'm not among them.

I am, however, having fun and feel I can devise a procedure to learn this skill which, I hope, will be applicable to future endeavors.

To this end I've devised an experimentation matrix to allow me to learn what flavors different honeys and yeasts produce. The goal is to limit variability along two axes (yeast and honey) and produce different test batches that will both help me refine my process and learn—to the best of my limited ability—what flavors I can produce.

If money, space and equipment were of no concern odd weeks would be for honey experiments and even weeks yeast experiments. I'd start a new one gallon batch every week with odd weeks using different honey with the same yeast and even weeks same honey different yeast.

In reality these resources are all limited. Local honeys may be obtained in 5-10 varieties but testing the sheer number of yeast strains would be prohibitively expensive.

Additionally this is a fairly aggressive experimentation pace if one also wants to age their Mead for any significant period. Considering I'm targeting sack strength Meads (an arbitrary target to be sure) it seems aging should take place for anywhere between 3 and 24 months.

This would no doubt lead to a lot of 1 gallon jugs aging in the basement which while fun would not be an efficient use of equipment. Admittedly 1 gallon jugs are not prohibitively expensive but at some point moving on to fermenting larger quantities of Mead would be more practical.

The plan therefore is to proceed with a new batch every other week experimenting with honey while keeping yeast the same. The experiments will be bottle aged and consumed at intervals of bottle date, 3 months and 6 months. This supposes I'll consistently produce 1 gallon batches.

This plan will of course be revised along the way.
